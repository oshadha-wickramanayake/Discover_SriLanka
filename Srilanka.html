Sri Lankan History

Sri Lanka's history spans over 3,000 years, with evidence of prehistoric human settlements dating back to 125,000 BCE. The island's strategic location made it a hub for ancient trade routes, connecting East and West.

The earliest recorded civilization dates to 543 BCE with the arrival of Prince Vijaya from India. The Anuradhapura Kingdom (377 BCE-1017 CE) established Buddhism as the national religion after Emperor Ashoka's son Mahinda introduced it in 247 BCE.

Portuguese explorers arrived in 1505, beginning 443 years of colonial rule that passed through Portuguese (1505-1658), Dutch (1658-1796), and British (1796-1948) administrations. Independence was finally achieved on February 4, 1948.

Key Historical Periods

Anuradhapura Period (377 BCE-1017 CE)

Established by King Pandukabhaya, this was Sri Lanka's first major kingdom. Key developments include:

  • Construction of massive stupas like Ruwanwelisaya (140m tall)
  • Advanced hydraulic systems with 30,000 reservoirs
  • The sacred Bodhi tree planted in 288 BCE - oldest known human-planted tree
Polonnaruwa Period (1017-1236)

The second capital after Chola invasions, featuring:

  • King Parakramabahu's sea-turtle shaped Parakrama Samudra reservoir
  • Gal Vihara's 4 monumental Buddha statues carved from granite
Portuguese Era (1505-1658)
  • Introduced Catholicism and cashew cultivation
  • Built coastal forts like Galle (now UNESCO site)
  • Began the "Burgher" Eurasian community
Dutch Period (1658-1796)
  • Established Roman-Dutch legal system still used today
  • Developed cinnamon plantations
  • Built canals and introduced Protestantism
British Rule (1796-1948)
  • Made English the official language
  • Established tea, rubber and coconut plantations
  • Built railways and modern infrastructure
  • Introduced the legislative council in 1833
Post-Independence (1948-present)

Key events in modern Sri Lankan history:

  • 1948: Independence as Dominion of Ceylon
  • 1956: Sinhala Only Act sparks ethnic tensions
  • 1972: Becomes Republic of Sri Lanka
  • 1983-2009: Civil war with LTTE Tamil Tigers
  • 2004: Indian Ocean tsunami kills 35,000
  • 2019: Easter Sunday bombings by Islamist extremists
  • 2022: Economic crisis leads to political unrest

Must-Visit Places in Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka has 8 UNESCO World Heritage Sites - 6 cultural and 2 natural.
Sigiriya Rock Fortress

This 5th-century "Lion Rock" palace complex rises 200m above the jungle. Features include:

  • Famous frescoes of the "Sigiriya Maidens"
  • Mirror Wall with ancient graffiti
  • Elaborate water gardens
  • Lion's Paw gateway

Best time to visit: January-April (dry season)

Temple of the Sacred Tooth Relic

Kandy's most sacred Buddhist site houses Buddha's left canine tooth. Highlights:

  • Daily rituals at 6:30am, 9:30am and 6:30pm
  • Annual Esala Perahera festival (July/August)
  • Golden canopy over the relic chamber
  • Kandy Lake views

Dress code: Cover shoulders and knees

Yala National Park

Sri Lanka's premier wildlife sanctuary with:

  • Highest leopard density in the world
  • 400+ elephants and 215 bird species
  • Ancient rock inscriptions
  • Coastal lagoons and monsoon forests

Safari times: 6-10am and 3-6pm daily

Anuradhapura

Ancient capital with remarkable ruins:

  • Sacred Bodhi Tree (oldest historical tree)
  • Jetavanaramaya - 3rd tallest ancient structure
  • Ruwanwelisaya dagoba (103m circumference)
  • Moonstone carvings at Isurumuniya

Tip: Rent a bicycle to explore the vast site

Ella

Mountain retreat with stunning scenery:

  • Ella Rock hike (2-3 hours)
  • Nine Arches Bridge (photogenic railway)
  • Ravana Falls and caves
  • Tea plantation tours

Must-do: Train ride from Kandy to Ella

Galle Fort

Dutch-colonial seaside fortress:

  • 17th-century ramparts and lighthouse
  • Boutique shops and cafes
  • National Maritime Museum
  • Whale watching tours (Nov-Apr)

Unique feature: Still a living community

Sri Lankan Handicrafts

Traditional Sri Lankan Crafts

Sri Lanka has a rich tradition of handicrafts that reflect its cultural heritage and artistic skills passed down through generations. These crafts are deeply connected to the island's Buddhist heritage and natural resources.

Ambalangoda Masks: These colorful ritual masks are carved from light balsa wood and used in traditional healing ceremonies (thovil) and kolam dance dramas. The 24 main mask types represent various deities and demons.

Temple Carvings: Intricately carved wooden pillars, doors and ceilings adorn ancient temples like Lankatilaka and Embekke Devalaya (14th century), featuring peacocks, dancers and wrestlers.

Batik: This wax-resist dyeing technique produces vibrant fabrics with traditional motifs like peacocks, elephants and temple designs. Best seen in the coastal towns of Ambalangoda and Hikkaduwa.

Dumbara Mats: Woven from hana grass and decorated with geometric patterns, these durable mats originate from the Dumbara valley near Kandy.

Traditional lacquer work from Matale involves coating wooden objects with resin from the lac insect (Kerria lacca), then polishing to a high gloss. Common items include bowls, boxes and ornaments in red, black and yellow designs.

Kandy is famous for its brass craftsmen who create oil lamps (panchadipastana), temple bells and statues using the lost-wax casting method. The Royal Brassware Factory in Kandy continues this 400-year tradition.

The Dediyawela village near Negombo produces distinctive black earthenware pots polished with pebbles. Traditional clay water pots (kalagedi) keep water cool naturally and are still used in rural areas.

Where to Buy Authentic Handicrafts

Laksala

Government-run stores with quality guaranteed products at fixed prices. Locations in Colombo, Kandy, Galle and airport.

Barefoot

Colombo's iconic store featuring contemporary designs using traditional techniques. Famous for their handloom fabrics.

Local Markets

Pettah Floating Market (Colombo), Kandy Market, and Galle Fort boutiques offer authentic crafts with bargaining expected.

Sri Lankan Culture & Traditions

Traditional Dance

Sri Lanka has three main dance forms:

  • Kandyan: Originating from the hill country, featuring elaborate Ves costumes with silver ornaments and headdresses. The ves dance is performed in the annual Kandy Esala Perahera.
  • Low Country: From coastal regions, featuring vibrant masks used in healing rituals (thovil) to exorcise demons causing illness.
  • Sabaragamuwa: From Ratnapura area, combining elements of both with distinctive drum rhythms.
Major Festivals
  • Sinhala & Tamil New Year (April): Marks the sun's movement from Pisces to Aries. Families prepare milk rice, light hearths at auspicious times, and play traditional games.
  • Vesak (May): Celebrates Buddha's birth, enlightenment and passing. Streets are decorated with lanterns (wesak koodu) and pandals depicting Jataka tales.
  • Kandy Esala Perahera (July/Aug): 10-day procession with caparisoned elephants, dancers and drummers honoring the Sacred Tooth Relic.
Cuisine

Signature dishes include:

  • Rice and Curry: Steamed rice with 3-12 curries like dhal, jackfruit, eggplant and spicy sambols
  • Hoppers (appa): Bowl-shaped pancakes made from fermented rice flour
  • Kottu Roti: Chopped roti stir-fried with vegetables, egg and meat
  • Lamprais: Dutch-influenced rice packet baked in banana leaves
Religious Practices
  • Buddhism (70%): Theravada Buddhism influences daily life with temple visits (viharaya), almsgiving to monks and observance of poya days
  • Hinduism (12%): Colorful kovils (temples) with gopuram towers, especially in Jaffna and Colombo
  • Islam (10%): Moor and Malay communities observe Ramadan and Eid
  • Christianity (7%): Colonial-era churches in Colombo, Galle and Jaffna
Cultural Highlights
  • Ayurveda: 3,000-year-old traditional medicine system using herbs, oils and massage
  • Elephant Pageants: Tuskers in elaborate costumes lead temple processions
  • Wedding Customs: Sinhalese brides wear Kandyan osari with 21 decorative ornaments
  • New Year Rituals: Auspicious times for cooking, transactions and returning to work
  • Architecture: Blend of ancient stupas, colonial buildings and modern designs
  • Puppetry: Traditional string puppets (Rukada) used for folk tales
  • Greetings: "Ayubowan" (may you live long) with palms together

Traditional Clothing

Women's Attire
  • Sari (Redda Hatte): 5.5m fabric draped with pleats at the waist
  • Osari (Kandyan Sari): More elaborate with frilled jacket and decorative sash
  • Half-Sari: Worn by unmarried girls with separate bodice and skirt
Men's Attire
  • Sarong: Checked or batik cloth wrapped around the waist
  • National Dress: White sarong with long-sleeved shirt and Nehru jacket
  • Temple Wear: White cloth covering shoulders and legs

Disasters & Civil War in Sri Lanka

Natural Disasters

Sri Lanka's tropical climate and geographical location make it vulnerable to several natural hazards:

2004 Indian Ocean Tsunami
  • Date: December 26, 2004
  • Deaths: 35,322 confirmed
  • Displaced: 516,150 people
  • Damage: $1.5 billion (5% of GDP)
  • Worst affected: East and South coasts including Ampara, Galle and Matara
2017 Floods and Mudslides
  • Date: May 2017
  • Deaths: 224 people
  • Displaced: 600,000 from their homes
  • Cause: Heavy monsoon rains (384mm in 24 hours)
  • Worst affected: Kalutara, Ratnapura and Galle districts
Other Notable Disasters
  • 1978 Cyclone: Killed 915 people
  • 2016 Drought: Affected 1.2 million people
  • 2021 MV X-Press Pearl: Worst marine ecological disaster with chemical spill

Sri Lankan Civil War (1983-2009)

The civil war was an armed conflict between the Sri Lankan government and the Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam (LTTE) seeking an independent Tamil state in the north and east.

Key Statistics
  • Duration: July 23, 1983 - May 18, 2009 (25 years)
  • Casualties: Estimated 80,000-100,000 deaths
  • Displaced: 800,000 at peak (2001)
  • Child Soldiers: LTTE recruited 6,000-18,000
  • Suicide Attacks: LTTE pioneered modern suicide bombing tactics
Major Events
  • 1983: Black July riots spark full-scale war
  • 1987-90: Indian Peace Keeping Force intervention
  • 2002: Norwegian-brokered ceasefire
  • 2009: Government's final offensive kills LTTE leader Prabhakaran
Aftermath
  • Continued ethnic tensions and human rights allegations
  • Ongoing landmine clearance in former conflict zones
  • Slow return of displaced Tamil populations
  • Economic development in former war-torn areas

Resilience and Recovery

Despite these challenges, Sri Lanka has shown remarkable resilience:

Tsunami Recovery
  • Rebuilt 98% of destroyed homes by 2010
  • Established early warning systems
  • Developed coastal buffer zones
Post-War Developments
  • Reconstructed roads and infrastructure in Northern Province
  • Demining of 2,000+ sq km of former conflict areas
  • Tourism revival in Jaffna and Eastern provinces
Current Challenges
  • Addressing wartime missing persons cases
  • Reconciliation between communities
  • Economic recovery from 2022 crisis

Vega EVX - Sri Lanka's Electric Supercar

Sri Lanka's Automotive Marvel

The Vega EVX is Sri Lanka's first electric supercar, developed by Vega Innovations, a subsidiary of the Codegen Group. Unveiled at the 2020 Geneva Motor Show, it represents a technological leap for Sri Lanka's automotive industry.

Development Team
  • Lead Designer: Harsha Subasinghe
  • Engineering: 100% Sri Lankan team
  • Testing: Done at Sri Lanka's only FIA-certified track in Pannala
Innovation Highlights
  • First EV with dual motor AWD system from South Asia
  • Patented battery cooling technology
  • Carbon fiber body panels manufactured locally
  • AI-powered driver assistance systems

Technical Specifications

Performance
  • Top Speed: 240 km/h (150 mph)
  • Acceleration: 0-100 km/h in 3.1 seconds
  • Power: 804 HP (600 kW)
  • Torque: 760 Nm (560 lb-ft)
Battery & Range
  • Battery Capacity: 55 kWh
  • Range: 250-300 km (155-186 miles)
  • Charging: 80% in 30 mins (DC fast charge)
  • Battery Type: Lithium-ion with liquid cooling
Dimensions
  • Length: 4,650 mm (183 in)
  • Width: 2,050 mm (81 in)
  • Height: 1,200 mm (47 in)
  • Weight: 1,800 kg (3,968 lbs)

Design Features

  • Exterior: Aerodynamic design with active rear wing and butterfly doors
  • Interior: Luxurious leather with Sri Lankan-inspired motifs
  • Infotainment: 15.6" touchscreen with Sri Lankan tea leaf-inspired UI
  • Lighting: LED lights shaped like traditional oil lamps
  • Wheels: 20" alloys with design inspired by Kandyan dancers

Market Position

  • Price: $400,000 (limited production)
  • Production: 25 units planned initially
  • Target Markets: Middle East, Europe, and collectors
  • Competitors: Rimac Nevera, Lotus Evija
  • Manufacturing: Hand-built in Colombo with 80% local content
